University of Central Florida

After I got a few years of CF development under my belt, I took a position as a project manager for a team of CF developers. In this role I learned a lot, I was responsible for the production of my team for better or worse. I stood up for my people when they were right, and taking the heat when they were not. I learned to judge talent, hiring people and, unfortunately, sometimes letting people go, always learning as I went. As things went along, and foreseeing that there would soon be little work for my team in the CF ecosystem, I began to migrate them to asp.net and C#. There were ups and downs, particularly resistance to change and the change from a dynamically typed, procedural language such as CF to the static typed object oriented C#. In time we made the change, and along the way I also learned C#. Given that I also knew the new language and was eager to get my hands dirty, I began to contribute code as well as leadership to the group. I took responsibility for infrastructure type work like authentication, authorization, data access and so on. – NOTE Speak more on the DOD stuff --

Eventually, I wanted to get more involved with the day to day development. My official responsibilities as the PM did not allow for this. At the same time, I was really interested in moving to Colorado so I could be involved in all the great outdoor activities. After a bit of discussion with the family, we decided to relocate. I took a position as a senior developer beginning on a maintenance team at Graebel. During that time, I played a key role in the re-development of one of their major applications tracking the movement of goods around the globe. After this work was released, the team lead got a promotion, and I was asked to take over many of her responsibilities, including working closely with the broad user-base, leading daily stand ups, and planning (with our analyst) for the upcoming sprints. During this time, I coded less, spending more time making sure things got done, tracking velocity, and on boarding new team members.

Graebel Companies

The next step in my career soon came (still with Graebel), I was offered the opportunity to become part of the greenfield development team. I joined an existing team consisting of five of perhaps the most talented developers I have ever worked with,. During this time I switched from web forms and WCF to MVC and REST. This was an extremely exciting time, and I took the opportunity to learn as much as humanly possible. I was a member of a team that gave me the opportunity to help develop the enterprise Api (using WebApi) tying our many desparent systems together through the data warehouse, domain driven design concepts, messaging, and an enterprise service bus. I also had a big role in development of the consuming enterprise portal, joining the data from all the systems together into a consolidated system for our users. Eventually, the organization decided to expand their use of Oracle, eliminating our project.

Toolwatch

My next position was to be with a small company that specialized in asset tracking. Their current app was a .net 2.0 winforms app, that was getting a bit long in the tooth. My task was to develop a RESTFul api and the rest of the server-side components to support their upcoming IPhone app. I dove in, developing a RESTful Api conforming to Level 3 of the Richardson Maturity Model using WebApi. The Api I provided is simple yet elegant. Though the Ios app itself is on its third re-write now, the api I provided remains the standard.
